# Why Open Scholar Exists

Open Scholar exists because knowledge deserves infrastructure that serves it, not exploits it.

## The Problem

Academic publishing became a business that charges creators to create, charges readers to read, and owns nothing but the locks on the doors. Art platforms optimize for engagement, not expression. Both treat work as content to be monetized, not contributions to be preserved.

The result: scholars perform for metrics, artists create for algorithms, and everyone's work sits behind walls—or disappears when platforms pivot.

## What We Built

Open Scholar is a peer-to-peer system for sharing scholarly and creative work. It runs on GrabNet, a content-addressed network where files live on the machines of people who care about them, not corporate servers that might vanish.

**What it does:**
- Lets anyone publish work under their own cryptographic identity
- Distributes that work across a network of peers
- Enables peer review appropriate to the type of work (empirical, theoretical, artistic, speculative, methodological)
- Preserves versions, drafts, and even failures
- Operates over Tor for those who need anonymity

**What it doesn't do:**
- Rank content algorithmically
- Show ads
- Optimize for engagement
- Hide moderation decisions
- Give institutions special powers
- Allow ownership transfer of works

## Core Beliefs

**Knowledge is not content.** It doesn't need virality. It needs preservation, context, and honest evaluation.

**Reputation should be earned per domain.** A brilliant physicist has no inherent authority on Renaissance painting. One identity, many reputational contexts.

**Process matters.** Drafts, failures, retractions, and dead ends are part of how we learn. We show them instead of hiding them.

**Small audiences are valid.** Five people who deeply engage with work are more valuable than five thousand who scroll past it. We don't optimize for attention.

**Negative results are results.** A null finding, a failed experiment, an artwork that intentionally resolves nothing—these deserve space alongside successes.

**Time is not the enemy.** Work can retire without being deleted. Old work can resurface when newly relevant. We don't chase novelty.

**Institutions adapt to us, not the other way around.** Universities, journals, and museums are welcome. They run nodes. They follow the same rules. They don't get special privileges.

## Sacred Constraints

These we will not violate, even if it costs growth:

1. **No advertising, ever.** Not now, not when we scale, not when we need money.

2. **No algorithmic ranking.** Discovery is slow and intentional. Trending does not exist.

3. **No ownership transfer of works.** You cannot sell your authorship. Your work remains yours.

4. **No opaque takedowns.** Every moderation action is visible, with reasoning, following stated procedure.

5. **No content involving minors.** This platform is for adults. Child exploitation content results in permanent bans, forfeiture of all works to the network, and reporting to authorities.

## Who This Is For

- Researchers who want their work accessible and preserved
- Artists who want process to be visible alongside product
- Writers, theorists, and thinkers working outside institutions
- Anyone who believes knowledge shouldn't be locked behind paywalls or lost to platform shutdowns

## Who This Is Not For

- People seeking viral reach
- Institutions wanting control
- Anyone expecting algorithmic amplification
- Those who need to monetize attention

## When Things Get Hard

If you're reading this during a conflict, a fork decision, or a moment of doubt:

Remember that we built this to serve work, not growth. If a choice serves the work but hurts the platform, serve the work. If a choice helps the platform but compromises these principles, walk away from the choice.

The network belongs to the people running nodes. The code belongs to everyone. The works belong to their creators. None of it belongs to us.
